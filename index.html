Phaedra consistently improves reconstruction across PDE datasets, capturing fine details and precise magnitudes critical for scientific simulation. This is achieved by splitting the embeddings into shape and gain components, allowing for high-fidelity tokenization that retains physical properties, even in out-of-distribution scenarios. Analyzing the amplitude tokens reveals that their distribution closely matches a low-resolution version of the original image, while reconstructions without this information only capture high-frequency details, missing the overall structure and magnitude.

Abstract

Tokens are discrete representations that allow modern deep learning to scale by transforming high-dimensional data into sequences that can be efficiently learned. As existing tokenizers are designed for realistic visual perception, we investigate whether these are optimal for scientific images, which exhibit a large dynamic range and require token embeddings to retain physical properties. We propose Phaedra, inspired by classical shape-gain quantization and proper orthogonal decomposition. We demonstrate that Phaedra consistently improves reconstruction across a range of PDE datasets and shows strong out-of-distribution generalization to unknown PDEs and real-world Earth observation data.

Phaedra's amplitude tokens capture the low-frequency structure of the original image, while the shape tokens encode high-frequency details. This separation becomes clear as we modify the amplitude tokens to be zero or to follow some pattern (e.g., a radial ripple). When the amplitude tokens are set to zero, the reconstruction only captures high-frequency details, losing the overall structure and magnitude. In contrast, when the amplitude tokens follow a radial ripple pattern, the reconstruction reflects this pattern, demonstrating that the amplitude tokens effectively capture the low-frequency structure of the original image.
